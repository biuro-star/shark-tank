### CATEGORY A: FINANCIALS (Questions 1-7)

---

**Q1: "What are your total revenues?"**

- **Ideal answer:** "In 2025, BenchK did $2 million in global revenue. Poland is $1 million — 50%. Germany, Switzerland, Czech Republic, and Slovakia combined are $700,000 — 35%. And the US in year one did $300,000 — 15%."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Rounding up to "$2-3 million" or saying "close to $3 million." Stick to $2 million exactly.
- **Most likely Shark:** Mark Cuban

---

**Q2: "Are you profitable?"**

- **Ideal answer:** "We've been profitable for years with zero outside investors. I've never taken a dollar of outside money in 10 years. Every dollar of growth was funded by our own cash flow."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Saying "profitable since day one" — that is unverifiable. Say "profitable for years" instead.
- **Most likely Shark:** Kevin O'Leary

---

**Q3: "What's your US revenue right now?"**

- **Ideal answer:** "$300,000 in 2025 — our first year of active management. But the momentum is real: December, January, and February each hit $60,000. That's $720,000 annualized. Three consecutive months is a trend, not an anomaly."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Projecting beyond $720K annualized without data to back it up. Do not say "$1 million next year" unless specifically asked about projections.
- **Most likely Shark:** Robert Herjavec

---

**Q4: "How much have you personally invested?"**

- **Ideal answer:** "$450,000-plus of my own money into US operations. Zero outside investors in the entire 10-year history of BenchK. I put my family's money where my mouth is."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Inflating the number or being vague. "$450,000-plus" is verified — do not say "half a million."
- **Most likely Shark:** Kevin O'Leary

---

**Q5: "What are your margins?"**

- **Ideal answer:** "Our distributor margin is approximately 50%. Our wholesale price runs $400 to $470 per unit, and retail prices range from $670 to $2,955 depending on the model and accessories."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Claiming specific net profit margin percentages if you have not verified them from ERP. If asked about net margins specifically, say: "I'd need to verify that exact number — I'd rather give you the accurate figure than guess."
- **Most likely Shark:** Mark Cuban

---

**Q6: "Walk me through your use of funds."**

- **Ideal answer:** "$200,000 to build US distribution — boutique studio chains, PT clinics, yoga, Pilates. $100,000 for marketing targeted at PT, chiropractic, and fitness studios. $50,000 for the BenchK PRO affiliate program where medical professionals earn 20% commission. And $50,000 for trade shows — IHRSA, SIBEC, and regional fitness events."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Being vague or saying "general growth." The breakdown is specific and rehearsed — deliver it with precision.
- **Most likely Shark:** Lori Greiner

**Q7: "You're valuing this at $2.67 million — how do you justify that?"**

- **Ideal answer:** "$2 million in global revenue, profitable, 10-year track record, zero debt to investors, and a US run rate of $720,000 in year one. At 1.3x revenue for a profitable, growing, bootstrapped product company — that's conservative, not aggressive."
- **Red flag to avoid:** Saying "I think we're worth more" or getting defensive. The valuation math is simple: $400K for 15% = $2.67M post-money. Let the numbers defend it.

## PART 2: 5 Killer Objections & How to Crush Them

---

### OBJECTION 1: "You're just a wall ladder company."

**Why they say it:** A Shark sees a 100-year-old product and thinks commodity.

**How to crush it:**

> "You're right — Swedish ladders are 100 years old. And nobody made money from them. Because nobody reinvented them. We did. BenchK is not a ladder — it is a modular vertical training wall. One wall. Five training methods. Zero floor space. We serve Pilates studios, PT clinics, fitness studios, hotels, and families — one product, five markets. We won the FIBO Innovation Award in 2022 for this. And we have $2 million in global revenue to prove it is not just a ladder."

**Key pivot:** Move from "ladder" to "training system" to "one product, five markets" to revenue proof.

---

### OBJECTION 2: "The US market is too different — what works in Poland won't work here."

**Why they say it:** European products often fail in the US. Different consumer behavior, different retail landscape.

**How to crush it:**

> "I understand the concern — and I had the same one. So I tested it. I opened a warehouse in Florida in August 2022 with one employee. I sold 800-plus units. I hit $60,000 per month for three consecutive months. $720,000 annualized run rate — in year one of active management. And 33% of those sales came from word of mouth. The product works here. The conversion works here. What I need is scale — and that is where a US partner changes everything."

**Key pivot:** Do not argue theory. Hit them with the US numbers that already exist. The 33% word-of-mouth stat proves product-market fit is not hypothetical.

---

### OBJECTION 3: "Your valuation is too high."

**Why they say it:** $2.67M post-money for a company doing $2M globally and $300K in the US.

**How to crush it:**

> "The post-money is $2.67 million on a company doing $2 million in global revenue. That is 1.3x revenue. Profitable. Bootstrapped. 10 years old. Zero debt. A $720,000 US annualized run rate in year one. And I have put $450,000 of my own money in. For a profitable, growing product company with proven international distribution — 1.3x revenue is conservative. Startups with zero revenue raise at 10x to 20x. I'm asking for 1.3x on real numbers."

**Key pivot:** Frame the 1.3x revenue multiple against what venture-backed startups raise at. This is not a startup valuation — it is an operator valuation.

---

### OBJECTION 4: "Why do you need a Shark?"

**Why they say it:** You are profitable, bootstrapped, growing. The classic "you don't need us" pushback — which is actually a compliment.

**How to crush it:**

> "You're right — I don't need money. I have my own. In 10 years I have never taken a dollar from anyone. But there is something I cannot buy: an American partner who knows American retail, American distribution, and American fitness. I can crack markets — I proved it in Poland. But it took me 10 years. With the right partner, we do it in two. I'm not asking you to take a chance. I'm asking you to join something that is already moving."

**Key pivot:** Turn the objection into your strongest moment. "Don't need money, need YOU" is the emotional peak of the pitch. Deliver the closer: "I'm not asking you to take a chance. I'm asking you to join something that is already moving."

---

### OBJECTION 5: "You don't have US traction."

**Why they say it:** $300K in a $2M business feels like US is an afterthought.

**How to crush it:**

> "Actually — $300,000 is 15% of global revenue in our very first year of active management. Before I moved here, BenchK ran on autopilot from Poland with one employee in a warehouse. Since I arrived with my family in November 2025, we hit $60,000 per month — three consecutive months. That is $720,000 annualized. Over 800 units sold to real US customers: Praxis Pilates in NYC, a PT clinic in Murrieta, 10Ten Fitness right here. One out of every three US customers came from word of mouth. That is not zero traction. That is product-market fit."

**Key pivot:** Reframe $300K from "small" to "first year, pre-founder-on-the-ground." Then hit the run rate, the customer names, and the 33% word-of-mouth conversion. Those numbers silence the objection.

## PART 3: The Question You WANT Them to Ask

---

### "How did you build a million-dollar business in Poland if you'd never lived there and didn't speak the language?"

**Why this is your golden question:**

This unlocks the single most powerful story in your pitch — the one no other Shark Tank applicant can tell. It connects all three core insights in one answer.

**The answer:**

> "In 2015, my family moved from Ukraine to Poland. I had never lived there. I didn't speak Polish. I didn't know anyone in business. I started BenchK and reached out to 100 European companies. 99 said no. One said yes.
>
> From that one yes, I built a $1 million business. I learned the language. I learned the culture. I built a factory from scratch in 2020 — 25 suppliers, three months, during a pandemic. I won an Innovation Award. I grew to $2 million globally with zero outside investors.
>
> That is my superpower — I crack new markets. I have done it once. I am doing it again in America. Same product. Same playbook. The US market is 8 times bigger than Poland. And I already have $720,000 annualized in year one.
>
> Sometimes the best thing that happens to you is everybody saying you can't."

**Why it wins:**

- Proves adaptability — you have already done the hardest thing once
- Defuses the "foreign founder" objection — foreignness IS the competitive advantage
- Demonstrates operator grit — 99 rejections, built it anyway
- Sets up the replication logic — same playbook, 8x bigger market
- Delivers the emotional punch line — "sometimes the best thing that happens to you is everybody saying you can't"
- No other applicant in Shark Tank history has this exact story

**How to steer toward this question:**

In the video, the Poland story is already in the script (Section 5). But if preparing for a live callback, drop the hook early:

> "I built a million-dollar business in a country I had never lived in..."

Any Shark will interrupt with: "Wait — what do you mean you'd never lived there?"

That is your door. Walk through it.
