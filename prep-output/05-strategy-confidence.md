Emily Watrobsky, as Casting Associate, is the first gatekeeper. Her job is to identify submissions that will (a) produce compelling television and (b) present a legitimate business the Sharks will engage with seriously.

The casting team watches thousands of videos. Most are eliminated in the first 30-60 seconds. What survives:

1. **Immediate visual clarity** — The product is visible, tangible, and understandable within 15 seconds. BenchK on the wall at 10Ten Fitness delivers this instantly.

2. **A founder who is watchable** — Not "polished" in the TV host sense. Watchable means genuine, energetic, and confident. An accent is an asset here — it signals authenticity and a real story.

5. **A product that demonstrates well on camera** — Wall ladders are inherently visual. Someone training on one is immediately compelling. Most Shark Tank products are small consumer goods — a full wall training system is visually dramatic and different.

Sit alone. Phone off. Close your eyes.

**Minute 1:** See the room at 10Ten Fitness. The wall ladder is behind you. The camera is in front of you. The light is set. You are standing. Your lavalier mic is clipped. Everything is ready.

**Minute 4:** See yourself delivering the closer. "I'm not asking you to take a chance. I'm asking you to join something that is already moving." You believe this because it is true. You have the revenue to prove it.

**Minute 5:** See the video file being uploaded. It is done. It is strong. You did your job. Now it is in their hands — and the work you have already done speaks for itself.

### The Operator Mindset

You are not auditioning. You are not performing. You are presenting a business report to potential partners.

Think about your JCB days. When you walked into a construction site to sell a $300,000 excavator, you did not "perform." You presented the machine, the economics, the service network, and the ROI. The customer either saw the value or they did not. Your job was clarity, not entertainment.

This is the same. The product is real. The numbers are real. The story is real. Your only job is to communicate clearly and let the facts carry the weight.

### How to channel nervousness into energy

Nervousness and excitement are the same physiological state — elevated heart rate, adrenaline, heightened focus. The only difference is the label you put on it.

Before the camera rolls, do not tell yourself "calm down." Instead tell yourself: "This energy is useful. I am going to put it into my voice and my conviction."

Practical steps:
- Stand up straight — posture affects vocal projection and confidence
- Take three slow breaths through your nose before the first take
- Speak to the camera like you are speaking to ONE person across a table — not an audience
- If your hands want to move, let them — natural gestures read as authenticity on camera

### The 3 Things to Tell Yourself Before the Camera Rolls

1. **"I built this."** You are not pitching someone else's idea. You built a factory. You built distribution in 5 countries. You built $2M in revenue. No one can take that from you, and no one can question it.

2. **"The numbers are the pitch."** If the words are imperfect, the numbers are not. $2M revenue. $720K US run rate. 33% word-of-mouth. 10 years. Zero investors. These facts do the heavy lifting.

3. **"I have done harder things than this."** You sold $300K machines to skeptical buyers. You built a business in a country where you did not speak the language. You relocated your family across the Atlantic. A 5-minute video is not the hardest thing you have done.

### What to do if you mess up mid-take

This is important: messing up is normal. Professional TV hosts do 5-10 takes of every segment. Here is the protocol:

1. **Stop talking.** Do not try to recover mid-sentence.
2. **Take one breath.** Not dramatic — just one normal breath.
3. **Say to the videographer: "Let me take that section again."**
4. **Pause for 3 seconds** (gives the editor a clean cut point).
5. **Restart the section from the top of that segment, not from the beginning of the video.**

The final video will be edited. Nobody will see the mistakes. The only version that matters is the one that gets submitted.

Give yourself permission to do 3-4 takes of any section. The best one gets used. The rest disappear.
