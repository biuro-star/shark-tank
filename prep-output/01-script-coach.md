## 1. SECTION-BY-SECTION PRACTICE PLAN

### SECTION 1 — Hook + Product Introduction (0:00 - 0:30 = 30 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver:**

> "Hi Sharks. My name is Vadym Zemlianyi. I'm Polish — with Ukrainian origins."
>
> "I'm asking for $400,000 for 15% equity in my company — BenchK."
>
> "We reinvented the 100-year-old Swedish ladder."
>
> "BenchK Vertical Training Wall. One wall. Five training methods. Zero floor space."

**Delivery notes:**
- Start slow. First 5 seconds set the tone for the entire video.
- Pause 1 full second after "My name is Vadym Zemlianyi" — let them absorb the name.
- "I'm Polish — with Ukrainian origins" — say this like a statement of pride, not an explanation. Dash = 0.5 second pause.
- Speed up slightly on the product description. "One wall. Five training methods. Zero floor space." — punch each phrase. Short pause between each.
- Energy: 7/10. Confident and warm. Not shouting.

**Body language:**
- Stand tall, shoulders back. Hands relaxed at sides for the greeting.
- On "BenchK Vertical Training Wall" — gesture toward the wall behind you with one open hand.
- On "One wall. Five training methods. Zero floor space." — hold up one finger on "One," open hand on "Five," flat hand sweep on "Zero." These are your signature gestures for this line.
- Direct eye contact with camera = eye contact with Sharks. Do not look away during the introduction.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Rushing the name and identity line (your accent makes it hard to catch if you rush)
- Saying "I'm Ukrainian" or "I'm Polish" alone (must be the combined phrase)
- Mumbling the Ask amount — "$400,000 for 15%" must be crisp and clear
- Looking at the BenchK wall instead of the camera when you introduce yourself

---

### SECTION 2 — Product Demo + Markets (0:30 - 1:10 = 40 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver:**

> "Modular. Premium. Commercial-grade."
>
> "Used by Pilates studios, PT clinics, hotels, and families."
>
> "One product. Five markets."

**Delivery notes:**
- This is the SHOW section. You should be moving, touching the wall, demonstrating.
- "Modular. Premium. Commercial-grade." — deliver these three words like they cost money. Slow. Each word gets its own beat.
- When listing the five markets, tick them off with slight pauses between each.
- Pace: medium (130 WPM). Let the product speak. Your body is doing half the work here.

**Body language:**
- Turn to the wall. Touch it. Pull a bar out if possible. Show modularity.
- Step slightly left/right to show scale of the wall.
- When you say "families" — soften your voice slightly and gesture outward, as if pointing to a home.
- Keep movements smooth and controlled. No jerky motions.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Talking about the product too technically (no engineering specs on camera)
- Saying "patented" — BenchK does not hold a patent on the ladder design
- Turning your back fully to camera while demonstrating
- Spending too long on demo and eating into Section 3 time

---

### SECTION 3 — Revenue + Numbers + Dylan Testimonial (1:10 - 2:25 = 75 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver:**

> "$2 million in global revenue."
>
> "Poland — $1 million. Germany, Switzerland, Czech Republic, Slovakia — $700,000. USA year one — $300,000."
>
> "$60,000 each month — December, January, February — three months in a row. That's a $720,000 annualized run rate."
>
> "And listen to this — one out of every three customers came from word of mouth. 33% conversion. No advertising. Just the product."
>
> "Sharks — meet Dylan. He owns this studio."

**Delivery notes:**
- This is the PROOF section. Energy shifts to authoritative.
- "$2 million in global revenue" — pause after. Let it land. Sharks are doing math in their heads.
- Revenue breakdown: deliver like a report. Clean. No stumbling. Practice these numbers until they are automatic.
- "$60,000 each month" — slow down here. This is the killer number. Hold up three fingers. "Three months in a row" — say it like it means something, because it does. Trend, not anomaly.
- "33% conversion" — slight smile when you say this. You know it is a great number.
- Transition to Dylan: soften tone. Turn to him. This is a human moment.
- Pace: 120 WPM on numbers (slower than normal — let them digest), 140 WPM on the word-of-mouth line (build excitement).

**Body language:**
- Stand still when delivering revenue numbers. Stability = credibility.
- Hold up fingers for counts (3 months, 33%).
- When transitioning to Dylan: turn body 45 degrees toward him, open hand gesture to introduce him.
- While Dylan speaks: look at HIM, not the camera. Nod naturally. Do NOT interrupt even if he pauses.
- After Dylan finishes: "Thanks, brother." + shoulder tap. Turn back to camera.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Saying "sold in 30 countries" — say "strong distribution in 5 EU countries + online across Europe"
- Mixing up the revenue numbers or percentages
- Interrupting Dylan or finishing his sentences
- Looking at the camera while Dylan speaks (look at Dylan)
- Saying "great answer" to Dylan (sounds scripted)
- Forgetting to give the lavalier mic to Dylan if only one is available

---

### SECTION 4 — Origin Story + JCB Background (2:25 - 3:15 = 50 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver:**

> "Before BenchK, I spent 9 years selling JCB construction machines. 250 machines. $90,000 to $300,000 each. Tens of millions in B2B sales."
>
> "I learned how premium global brands operate at scale."
>
> "In 2015, my family moved to Poland. I founded BenchK. I went to 100 European companies. 99 said no."
>
> "Sometimes the best thing that happens to you... is everybody saying you can't."

**Delivery notes:**
- This section tells your STORY. Pace slows down. Energy drops to 6/10 — this is personal.
- JCB numbers: deliver factually. You are establishing credibility as a salesman, not bragging.
- "99 said no" — pause before this. Let the rejection register.
- THE PUNCH LINE: "Sometimes the best thing that happens to you... is everybody saying you can't." — This line needs a 1-second pause at the ellipsis. Drop your voice slightly on "you can't." This is not shouting. This is a man who has been through it.
- Pace: 110-120 WPM. Slowest section of the video so far.

**Body language:**
- Stand grounded. Feet shoulder-width apart.
- On "250 machines" — slight nod, matter-of-fact.
- On "99 said no" — small head shake, almost a smile. You survived it.
- On the punch line — look directly into camera. Do not move. Let the words do the work.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Getting emotional or breaking down (emotion is fine, breakdown is not)
- Saying "I escaped from Ukraine" — say "my family moved to Poland"
- Mentioning Russia, war, or political topics
- Rushing through the punch line — it needs breathing room
- Saying "I really need this" or any begging language

---

### SECTION 5 — Operator Identity + Foreign Market Mastery (3:15 - 4:15 = 60 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver (Insight #1):**

> "I'm Polish with Ukrainian origins. I built a $1 million business in Poland — a country I had never lived in, in a language I didn't speak, with people whose mentality I had to learn from zero."
>
> "If I cracked Poland from zero — what makes you think I won't crack America?"
>
> "I don't run a startup. I run a factory."
>
> "10 years old. Profitable. Award-winning. Zero outside investors."

**Delivery notes:**
- Energy rises back up to 7/10. You are building the argument now.
- The Poland story is your secret weapon. Most Shark Tank founders never had to crack a foreign market. You did it TWICE.
- "I don't run a startup. I run a factory." — This is punch line #2. Deliver it with a pause before and after. It reframes everything.
- "Zero outside investors" — let this sit. It means you are not desperate.
- Pace: 130 WPM. Conversational but purposeful.

**Body language:**
- Step forward slightly on "what makes you think I won't crack America?" — subtle move toward camera = confidence.
- On "I run a factory" — plant your feet. Do not move. Factory = solid.
- Count off on fingers: "10 years old. Profitable. Award-winning. Zero outside investors." — four points, four fingers.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Overclaiming: do not say "made in Poland and the United States" (US production not started)
- Losing the thread between the Poland story and the factory statement
- Rushing "I run a factory" — it is a punch line, treat it like one
- Saying "we're profitable since day one" (unverifiable — say "profitable for years")

---

### SECTION 6 — Replication Logic (4:15 - 4:55 = 40 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver (Insight #2):**

> "We achieved $1 million in Poland. 38 million people. The US market — 340 million. 8 times bigger."
>
> "Same product. Same playbook. Same operator."
>
> "The barrier in any new market is not awareness — it is trial. Once they see it, touch it, train on it — they buy."
>
> "33% word-of-mouth conversion proves it. Poland proved it. $60,000 a month in year one proves it."

**Delivery notes:**
- Energy: 8/10. This is the intellectual crescendo. You are showing them the math.
- "8 times bigger" — hold up fingers or gesture broadly.
- "Same product. Same playbook. Same operator." — three short sentences. Punch each one. Pause between each.
- "See it, touch it, train on it — they buy" — speed up through the list, then STOP on "they buy." Hard period.
- Pace: 140 WPM. Fastest section. You are building momentum toward the Ask.

**Body language:**
- Use hands to show scale — small gesture for Poland, wide gesture for USA.
- On "Same operator" — point to yourself with thumb.
- On "they buy" — nod once. Definitive.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Getting the population numbers wrong (Poland ~38M, USA ~340M)
- Sounding like a math lecture — keep it human
- Forgetting to connect the 33% conversion back to the trial argument
- Losing energy right before the Ask (this section feeds into the close)

---

### SECTION 7 — The Ask + Smart Money (4:55 - 5:15 = 20 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver (Insight #3):**

> "In 10 years, I've never taken outside investment. $450,000 of my own money in the US."
>
> "I don't need your money — I have my own. I need YOU. American mindset. American network. American doors I can't open from a warehouse in Florida."
>
> "$400,000 for 15%."

**Delivery notes:**
- Energy: 9/10. This is the peak. You are telling billionaires you do not need their money. That takes confidence.
- "I don't need your money" — pause after. Sharks will react internally. Then: "I need YOU." — direct. Personal. Point at the camera.
- "American mindset. American network. American doors." — triple structure. Each one slightly louder than the last.
- "$400,000 for 15%" — calm. Factual. After all that emotion, the number is clean.
- Pace: 130 WPM. Controlled power. Not shouting.

**Body language:**
- On "I need YOU" — point at camera with index finger. One deliberate point.
- On "$450,000 of my own money" — touch your chest briefly.
- On "$400,000 for 15%" — open both hands, palms up. Offering, not begging.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Saying "please give me a chance" or "I really need this" (kills the entire smart money frame)
- Mumbling the Ask — "$400,000 for 15%" must be crystal clear
- Looking away from camera during the Ask
- Rushing through "American mindset, American network, American doors" — each one needs its own beat

---

### SECTION 8 — Closer (5:15 - 5:30 = 15 seconds)

**Key lines to deliver (word-for-word — memorize exactly):**

> "BenchK is not a startup. 10 years old. Profitable. Award-winning."
>
> "I'm not asking you to take a chance. I'm asking you to join something that is already moving."
>
> "Lift Yourself. Sharks — let's lift America together."

**Delivery notes:**
- Energy: 10/10 at "already moving" then drop to 8/10 for the final tagline. Warm, not aggressive.
- "I'm not asking you to take a chance" — slow. Deliberate. Pause at the period.
- "I'm asking you to join something that is already moving" — slightly faster, lean into "already moving." This is the climax of the entire video.
- "Lift Yourself" — company tagline. Say it with pride, but calm.
- "Sharks — let's lift America together." — Final line. Smile. Hold eye contact with camera for 2 full seconds after you finish. Do NOT look away.
- Pace: 110 WPM. Slow and deliberate. Every word matters.

**Body language:**
- Stand completely still. No fidgeting.
- On "already moving" — smallest possible nod.
- On "Lift Yourself" — hands at sides or one hand lightly on the BenchK wall.
- After "let's lift America together" — hold position. Smile. Do not move until the director says cut. The editor needs a clean ending.

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Adding words after "together" — that is the last word. Stop.
- Breaking eye contact before the director calls cut
- Rushing the closer because you are relieved it is almost over
- Changing any word of the closer — this is memorized verbatim

## 2. PACE GUIDE — Words Per Minute by Section

| Section | Time | WPM Target | Why |
|---------|------|-----------|-----|
| 1 — Hook + Product Intro | 0:00-0:30 | 120 WPM | Slow start. Let name and identity land clearly. Accent needs space. |
| 2 — Product Demo | 0:30-1:10 | 130 WPM | Medium. Body is doing half the work. Let demo breathe. |
| 3 — Revenue + Dylan | 1:10-2:25 | 120 WPM (numbers) / 140 WPM (word-of-mouth excitement) | Numbers need time to register. Speed up on the 33% conversion for energy. |
| 4 — Origin Story | 2:25-3:15 | 110 WPM | Slowest section. Emotional. Punch lines need air. |
| 5 — Operator Identity | 3:15-4:15 | 130 WPM | Conversational authority. Building the argument. |
| 6 — Replication Logic | 4:15-4:55 | 140 WPM | Fastest section. Momentum. Math. Building toward the Ask. |
| 7 — The Ask | 4:55-5:15 | 130 WPM | Controlled power. Not too fast, not too slow. |
| 8 — Closer | 5:15-5:30 | 110 WPM | Slowest finish. Every. Word. Counts. |

**Reference:** Average English conversation = 130 WPM. News anchor = 150 WPM. Slow dramatic speech = 100 WPM.

**Non-native speaker adjustment:** When you feel you are speaking "too slow," you are probably at the right speed. Your instinct will be to speed up when nervous — resist it. Slower is clearer. Clearer is more credible.

## 4. THE 5 LINES THAT MUST BE PERFECT

These 5 lines must be delivered word-for-word. No improvisation. No "close enough." Memorize them exactly as written.

---

**LINE 1 — The Identity Statement**

> "I'm Polish — with Ukrainian origins."

Why it must be exact: Legal, cultural, and strategic precision. Any variation ("I'm Ukrainian," "I'm from Ukraine," "I'm Polish") creates problems with the vetting team, the visa narrative, or the identity framing. The dash is a pause.

---

**LINE 2 — Punch Line #1 (Rejection)**

> "Sometimes the best thing that happens to you... is everybody saying you can't."

Why it must be exact: This is the emotional peak of the origin story. The ellipsis is a real pause (1 second). "You can't" — not "you shouldn't" or "you won't" or "they don't believe in you." "Can't" is final. It is stronger.

---

**LINE 3 — Punch Line #2 (Factory)**

> "I don't run a startup. I run a factory."

Why it must be exact: This reframes BenchK in two sentences. "Startup" is what Sharks hear 200 times a season. "Factory" is what they almost never hear. The contrast is the point. Do not say "I'm not a startup" — say "I don't RUN a startup." The verb matters.

---

**LINE 4 — Punch Line #3 / Closer Core**

> "I'm not asking you to take a chance. I'm asking you to join something that is already moving."

Why it must be exact: This is the single most important line of the entire video. "Take a chance" vs "join something" — the reframe does all the work. "Already moving" — not "that works" or "that's growing" or "that's successful." "Already moving" implies momentum. It implies the train is leaving the station. Get on or miss it.

---

**LINE 5 — Final Words**

> "Lift Yourself. Sharks — let's lift America together."

Why it must be exact: "Lift Yourself" is the BenchK tagline. "Let's lift America together" ties the tagline to the Sharks, to the US market, and to partnership. The word "together" is the last word the Sharks hear. It must be "together" — not "with me" or "as partners."
