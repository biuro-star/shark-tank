## 2. FILLER WORDS TO ELIMINATE

Polish and Ukrainian speakers have specific filler patterns in English. These are yours to watch for.

---

### The Fillers

| Filler | Why You Do It | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| **"So..."** (at start of sentences) | Polish speakers use "No..." or "Wiec..." to start — "So" is the English equivalent | Start the sentence directly. Delete "So." Replace with silence. |
| **"You know..."** | Filler while thinking. Very common in Slavic-to-English speakers. | Pause silently. A pause looks confident. "You know" looks uncertain. |
| **"Actually..."** | Used to soften every statement. "Actually, we have $2 million..." | Delete it. Say the fact directly. "$2 million in global revenue." |
| **"Basically..."** | Same as "actually" — a cushion word. "Basically, we sell..." | Delete it. You are not summarizing. You are stating. |
| **"Like..."** | Picked up from American casual English. "We have, like, 800 units..." | Delete it. Numbers do not need "like." |
| **"I mean..."** | Used to rephrase. "I mean, the product is..." | Say it right the first time. If you need to rephrase, just pause and restart. |
| **"Let's say..."** | Hedging. "Let's say we have $2 million..." | You DO have $2 million. State it as fact. "We have $2 million." |
| **"How to say..."** / **"How do you say..."** | Searching for English word mid-sentence | Pause. Find the word. Say it. The pause is invisible on camera. The filler is not. |
| **"Uh..." / "Um..."** | Universal filler, but Slavic speakers lean on it heavily when translating in their head | Replace with a breath. Inhale through nose. Exhale. Then speak. The audience reads a breath as "this person is thinking carefully." They read "uh" as "this person does not know." |
| **"More or less"** | Polish "mniej wiecej" — hedging precision | Be precise or say "approximately." "More or less" sounds unsure. On Shark Tank, unsure = uninvested. |
| **"Of course"** | Polish "oczywiscie" — used too often as filler agreement | Use sparingly. Once in the entire video, maximum. Replace with a nod or "Yes." |

### The Fix: The Silent Pause Technique

When you feel a filler coming:

1. Close your mouth.
2. Breathe in through your nose (0.5 seconds).
3. Open your mouth.
4. Say the next real word.

That half-second of silence is more powerful than any filler. On camera, silence = confidence. Fillers = searching.

**Practice drill:** Record yourself telling your BenchK story for 2 minutes. Count every filler. Then do it again. Target: zero fillers, even if it means pausing 10 times. The pauses will feel awkward to you. They will look powerful to the Sharks.

## 4. RHYTHM AND PACING TRICKS

Your accent and your third-language delivery actually become an advantage when you control the rhythm. Here is how.

---

### The Four Rules of Pacing

**RULE 1: Slow down for numbers. Always.**

Numbers are where non-native speakers lose their audience. When you say "$720,000 annualized run rate" quickly, it becomes a blur of unfamiliar sounds. When you say it slowly, each number lands.

Practice pattern:
```
"Sixty thousand dollars..."      [pause 1 second]
"...each month..."               [pause 0.5 seconds]
"...December, January, February..." [pause 1 second]
"...three months in a row."      [pause 1.5 seconds — let it land]
"That is a seven-hundred-twenty-thousand-dollar annualized run rate."
```

**RULE 2: Pause BEFORE the important line, not after.**

Most speakers pause after a big statement. The power move is to pause BEFORE it. The silence creates anticipation.

```
Wrong:  "I don't run a startup. I run a factory." [pause]
Right:  [pause] "I don't run a startup." [beat] "I run a factory."
```

```
Wrong:  "Sometimes the best thing that happens to you is everybody saying you can't." [pause]
Right:  "Sometimes the best thing that happens to you..." [1-second pause] "...is everybody saying you can't."
```

**RULE 3: Use the "3-3-3 Breathing" between sections.**

Between each section of the script (there are 8), take one breath cycle:

- 3 seconds: inhale through nose
- 3 seconds: hold
- 3 seconds: exhale through mouth

This does three things:
1. Resets your pace (prevents speeding up from nerves)
2. Gives the editor a clean cut point
3. Drops your voice pitch slightly (deeper = more authoritative)

You will feel like the pause is too long. It is not. On camera, 3 seconds between sections looks like a confident transition.

**RULE 4: End sentences DOWN, not UP.**

Polish and Ukrainian speakers often end English sentences with a rising tone (going up at the end), which makes statements sound like questions.

```
Wrong:  "$2 million in global revenue?" ← sounds uncertain
Right:  "$2 million in global revenue." ← sounds factual
```

```
Wrong:  "I'm asking for $400,000 for 15%?" ← sounds like you are asking permission
Right:  "I'm asking for $400,000 for 15%." ← sounds like you know your worth
```

Practice: Say every number with your voice going DOWN at the end. Down = statement. Up = question. You are not asking. You are telling.

---

### Section-by-Section Pacing Notes

| Section | Pace Instruction | Breathing |
|---|---|---|
| S1 — Hook (0:00) | Start at 70% speed. Your name, your identity — slow and clear. Sharks are calibrating your accent in the first 5 seconds. Give them time. | Deep breath before "Hi Sharks." |
| S2 — Demo (0:30) | Medium pace. Your body is moving, so your words can relax. | Breath when you turn toward the wall. |
| S3 — Numbers (1:10) | SLOWEST on dollar amounts. Speed up on "33% conversion" for excitement contrast. | Breath before "$2 million in global revenue." Breath before Dylan transition. |
| S4 — Origin (2:25) | Slow. Personal. This is the emotional valley. Let words hang in the air. "99 said no" — pause 2 seconds before the punch line. | Breath before "Sometimes the best thing..." |
| S5 — Identity (3:15) | Medium-fast. You are building an argument. Conversational energy. | Breath before "I don't run a startup." |
| S6 — Replication (4:15) | Fastest section. Momentum. But still slow on "38 million" and "340 million." | Breath before "Same product. Same playbook." |
| S7 — The Ask (4:55) | Controlled. Not fast, not slow. "$400,000 for 15%" — measured and calm. | Breath before "I don't need your money." |
| S8 — Closer (5:15) | SLOWEST of all. Every word earns its space. Hold the final word "together" for a full second. | Breath before "BenchK is not a startup." |

### The Metronome Trick

Download a free metronome app. Set it to 60 BPM (one beat per second).

- Practice Section 4 (Origin Story) with one word per beat. This will feel absurdly slow. Good. It trains you to resist rushing.
- Practice Section 1 (Hook) with two words per beat. This is your target speaking speed for the opening.
- Practice Section 6 (Replication Logic) with three words per beat. This is your target for the fastest section.

After 3-4 runs with the metronome, your body will internalize the rhythm. Then turn it off and speak naturally. You will be slower and clearer without trying.

## 5. POWER PHRASES VS WEAK PHRASES

Ten examples of weak phrasing transformed into power phrasing. These are based on common patterns for Polish/Ukrainian speakers delivering business English.

---

| # | WEAK (do not say) | POWER (say this) | Why It Is Stronger |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "We are trying to enter the American market" | "We are in the American market. $60,000 a month proves it." | "Trying" = uncertain. You are not trying. You are doing. |
| 2 | "I think our product is very good" | "The product sells itself. 33% of customers come from word of mouth." | "I think" = opinion. Data = fact. Let the number speak. |
| 3 | "We have sold maybe around 800 units" | "800+ units sold in the US since 2022." | "Maybe around" = guessing. State the verified number. Period. |
| 4 | "I hope you will invest in us" | "I'm asking for $400,000 for 15%." | Hope = passive. Asking = active. State what you want. |
| 5 | "Our company is doing quite well in Europe" | "$2 million in global revenue. $1 million from Poland alone." | "Quite well" is vague. Numbers are not. |
| 6 | "I believe I can succeed in America" | "If I cracked Poland from zero, what makes you think I won't crack America?" | "I believe" is weak self-talk. A rhetorical question is a challenge. It is bold. |
| 7 | "We would really appreciate your help" | "I don't need your money. I need YOU. American mindset. American network. American doors." | "Appreciate your help" = polite but forgettable. "I need YOU" = direct and memorable. |
| 8 | "The product is quite versatile and can be used in many places" | "One product. Five markets. Pilates studios. PT clinics. Hotels. Home gyms. Commercial fitness." | "Quite versatile" = adjective. Listing the markets = proof. |
| 9 | "We have been in business for a long time" | "10 years. Profitable. Award-winning. Zero outside investors." | "A long time" = vague. "10 years" + proof points = credible. |
| 10 | "I put a lot of my own money into this" | "$450,000 of my own money in the US. Zero outside investors in 10 years." | "A lot" = undefined. $450K = real. Sharks respect specifics. |

### The Pattern

Every weak phrase has the same problem: **hedging.** Polish business culture values modesty and understatement. American business culture (especially Shark Tank) values directness and proof.

You are not bragging. You are reporting. There is a difference.

- Bragging: "We are the best wall training system in the world."
- Reporting: "$2 million in global revenue. 800 units in the US. 33% word-of-mouth conversion."

Report. Let the Sharks decide you are the best. They will.

## 6. VOICE WARM-UP ROUTINE (15 Minutes)

Do this Monday morning, May 4, before leaving for 10Ten Fitness. You can do it in the shower, in the car (parked), or in a quiet room.

---

### Minutes 0-3: BREATHING AND RESONANCE

**Exercise 1 — Diaphragm Activation (1 min)**
- Stand tall. Feet shoulder-width apart.
- Place one hand on your belly, one on your chest.
- Breathe in through your nose for 4 seconds. Your belly should push out. Your chest should NOT move.
- Hold for 2 seconds.
- Exhale through your mouth for 6 seconds, making a "ssssss" sound.
- Repeat 5 times.
- Why: This engages your diaphragm. Speaking from the diaphragm makes your voice deeper, louder, and steadier. Speaking from the throat (common when nervous) makes your voice thin and shaky.

**Exercise 2 — Humming (2 min)**
- Close your lips. Hum a low note. Feel the vibration in your chest.
- Slowly raise the pitch. Feel the vibration move to your nose, then your forehead.
- Come back down to the low note. Stay there.
- Hum the opening line of the script: "Hi Sharks. My name is Vadym Zemlianyi." (Just the melody, hummed, no words.)
- Hum it 5 times. Each time, feel the vibration in your chest.
- Why: Humming warms up your vocal cords gently. The low pitch is where your voice has the most authority.

---

### Minutes 3-7: ARTICULATION AND CLARITY

**Exercise 3 — Tongue Twisters for Polish/Ukrainian Speakers (2 min)**

Do each one 3 times slowly, then 3 times at speed:

1. **"Red leather, yellow leather"** — trains the L and R distinction (hard for Slavic speakers)
2. **"Three hundred thirty-three thousand"** — trains the TH sound you need for every number
3. **"She sells seashells by the seashore"** — trains the SH vs S distinction
4. **"Worldwide commercial-grade wall"** — trains W (not V) and the "shul" sound
5. **"Thirty-three percent word-of-mouth"** — this IS your script. Practice it as a tongue twister.

**Exercise 4 — The Cork Technique (2 min)**
- Put a wine cork (or pen cap or thumb) between your front teeth. Bite gently to hold it.
- Read the opening of the script aloud with the cork in your mouth:
  - "Hi Sharks. My name is Vadym Zemlianyi. I'm Polish, with Ukrainian origins."
- It will sound ridiculous. That is the point. Your mouth is forced to over-articulate.
- Remove the cork. Say the same lines again. You will sound dramatically clearer.
- Do this for Section 1 and Section 8 (the two memorized sections).
- Why: The cork forces your jaw, lips, and tongue to work harder. When you remove it, normal speech feels effortless and your diction is sharper.

---

### Minutes 7-10: PROJECTION AND VOLUME

**Exercise 5 — The Wall Test (1.5 min)**
- Stand 3 meters (10 feet) from a wall.
- Say your opening line as if the wall is a person who needs to hear you clearly:
  - "I'm asking for $400,000 for 15% equity in my company, BenchK."
- Now step back to 5 meters (16 feet). Say it again. Same volume? Louder? You need to fill the room.
- Your target volume for filming: loud enough to be heard clearly 5 meters away, without shouting. This is "presentation voice," not "conversation voice."

**Exercise 6 — Pitch Drop (1.5 min)**
- Say this line in your normal voice: "I don't run a startup. I run a factory."
- Now say it again, but drop your voice one note lower on "factory."
- Say it a third time, and drop your voice on BOTH "startup" and "factory."
- The lower pitch on key words signals authority. Sharks unconsciously trust deeper voices more.
- Practice the same pitch drop on: "$400,000 for 15%." Drop your voice on "fifteen percent." It sounds like you KNOW your value.

---

### Minutes 10-13: SPEED CONTROL

**Exercise 7 — The Slow-Fast-Slow Drill (3 min)**

Read this passage at three different speeds:

> "We achieved $1 million in Poland. 38 million people. The US market — 340 million. 8 times bigger. Same product. Same playbook. Same operator."

- **Round 1 (SLOW):** One word per second. Painfully slow. Feel every word.
- **Round 2 (FAST):** As fast as you can while still being clear. Find your ceiling.
- **Round 3 (RIGHT):** Find the middle. This is your presentation speed.

Repeat with:

> "I'm not asking you to take a chance. I'm asking you to join something that is already moving."

- Round 1: Slow. Every word deliberate.
- Round 2: Fast. Feel where it breaks down.
- Round 3: Right speed. This is the one you will use on camera.

---

### Minutes 13-15: FULL WARM-UP RUN

**Exercise 8 — The Confidence Run (2 min)**

- Stand tall. Shoulders back. Chin level (not up, not down).
- Take one deep diaphragm breath.
- Deliver Section 1 (Hook) at full performance energy. Do not hold back.
- Then deliver Section 8 (Closer) at full performance energy.
- These are your bookends. If they are warm and strong, the middle will follow.

**After the warm-up:**
- Drink room-temperature water (not cold — cold tightens vocal cords).
- Do not whisper or clear your throat aggressively (both strain your voice).
- Stay quiet for 5 minutes. Let your voice rest before the shoot begins.

## 7. "ACCENT IS AN ASSET" MINDSET

This is the most important section of this document. Read it the night before filming. Read it again in the morning.

---

### Your Accent Proves Your Story

Every word you say on camera carries the sound of three languages, two countries, and a decade of building something from zero. That sound is not a weakness. It is evidence.

When the Sharks hear your accent, they do not hear "this person cannot speak English well." They hear "this person learned English as a third language, moved to a foreign country, built a $2 million company, and is standing here delivering a pitch in a language that is not his own." That is the definition of an operator.

### What the Accent Communicates Without You Saying a Word

- **Resilience.** You learned Polish for business. Then English for America. Language barriers did not stop you.
- **Authenticity.** You are not a polished Silicon Valley founder reading from a script someone else wrote. You are the person who built this. Your voice proves it.
- **Global experience.** BenchK operates in 5+ countries. Of course the founder has an accent. It would be strange if he did not.
- **The story itself.** "I'm Polish, with Ukrainian origins" is not just a line in the script. Your accent IS that line, spoken in every word.

### Founders with Accents Who Won on Shark Tank

Accented founders regularly close deals on Shark Tank. The Sharks do not penalize accents. They penalize:
- Unclear numbers (fixable with practice)
- Low confidence (fixable with preparation)
- Overclaiming (fixable with discipline)
- Vague answers (fixable with the Numbers Cheat Sheet)

None of those are about accent. They are about preparation. And you are prepared.

### The Mindset Shift

| Old Thinking (Delete This) | New Thinking (Install This) |
|---|---|
| "My English is not good enough" | "My English is clear enough. And my accent tells my story." |
| "They won't understand me" | "If I speak slowly and clearly, they will understand every word. And my accent will make them remember me." |
| "I should try to sound American" | "I should sound like myself. A Polish-Ukrainian entrepreneur who built a $2 million company and flew to America to pitch it." |
| "I need to apologize for my accent" | "I never apologize. My accent is proof of everything I have accomplished." |
| "Other founders speak better English" | "Other founders have not built a business in three languages across two continents. I have." |

### What to Do If You Stumble on a Word During Filming

It will happen. Here is the protocol:

1. **Stop.** Do not try to power through a mangled word.
2. **Breathe.** One breath.
3. **Restart the sentence.** Not the word — the whole sentence. Cleanly.
4. **Keep going.** The editor will cut the stumble. That is what editors do.

You have 3-5 takes per section. Use them. Nobody delivers a perfect take on the first try, in any language.

### The Line That Captures It All

From your own script, Section 5:

> "I built a $1 million business in Poland — a country I had never lived in, in a language I did not speak, with people whose mentality I had to learn from zero."

That line is about Poland. But it is also about America. And it is also about this video. You are doing it again, right now, in English, on camera, in a country you moved to six months ago.

The accent does not weaken this line. The accent IS this line.

### Final Word

Vadym, you have sold 250 JCB machines in a language you learned as an adult. You have built distribution across 5 European countries from a standing start. You have moved your family across the Atlantic on an investor visa funded by your own money. You have generated $60,000 a month in a country where you have been living for less than a year.

English is your third language. The Shark Tank pitch is your next market to crack. And you already know how to crack markets.

Do not hide the accent. Do not soften it. Do not apologize for it.

Speak clearly. Speak slowly. Speak proudly.

The accent is not the obstacle. It is the proof.
